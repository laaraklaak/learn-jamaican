Lesson 2: Rules for Writing

Now that you know the sounds, let's look at the rules that bring them together. These simple rules will help you read and write Jamaican with confidence.

Core Principles

Rule 1: One Sound, One Symbol

This is the most important rule. The Jamaican writing system is phonetic, meaning we write words exactly as they sound. Each letter or letter combination has only one sound. Always.

Rule 2: No Double Consonants

We never double consonants in a word. This simplifies spelling greatly.

  • likl (not 'likkle')
  • beta (not 'better')
  • mata (not 'matter')

Structural Rules

Word Endings

One of the biggest differences from English is how we handle the ends of words. We often simplify them to match how we speak.

  • Dropping the final consonant: Words that end with a consonant cluster in English often drop the last letter.
    han (from 'hand'), sof (from 'soft'), kuol (from 'cold').
  • The '-ing' ending becomes '-in':
    taakin (talking), gwaan (going on), siin (seeing).
  • The '-er' ending becomes '-a':
    wata (water), tiicha (teacher), beta (better).

Hyphens Connect Ideas

We use hyphens to link short words that are spoken together as a single thought or function. This makes reading much smoother.

  • Verbs of being/doing:
    a-go (am/is/are going to), de-ya (am/is/are here).
    Example: Mi a-go iit. (I am going to eat.)
  • Possession:
    fi-mi (for me/mine), fi-yu (for you/yours).
    Example: A fi-mi buk dat. (That is my book.)

No Apostrophe 's' for Possession

To show who owns something, we simply put the owner before the item. There is no need for an apostrophe 's' like in English.

  • Di uman yaad (The woman's yard)
  • Pita kyar (Peter's car)

A GO vs. A-GO vs. AGO

The context of these words is important. They look similar but have very different meanings based on how they are written.

  • a go (two words): Means you are physically going somewhere.
    Mi a go a maakit tumaro.
  • a-go (hyphenated): Means you are "going to do" something.
    Mi a-go kuk som rais an piis fi dina.
  • ago (one word): Refers to a time in the past.
    Ten ier ago dem staat di bizniz.

Specific Letter Rules

The 'OR' Sound Exception

While most letters have one sound, the combination or can sometimes be pronounced with an er sound, especially when it is followed by a consonant.

'S' vs 'Z' at the End of Words

The letter you use at the end of a word depends on the sound. 'S' makes a soft, hissing sound (no vibration in your throat). 'Z' makes a hard, buzzing sound (you can feel the vibration).

Soft 'S' Sound (No Vibration)Hard 'Z' Sound (Vibration)
TangksVaibz
BaksBiiz
NuotsNuoz

Special Consonant Sounds

  • The 'HN' Nasal Sound: This sound always comes after a vowel and is made in your nose.
    weehn, iihn, duohn, kyaahn, waahn.
  • The 'NG' Sound: This is a single sound, different from a hard 'g'.
    sing, ting. In words like fing-ga or mang-go, you hear both the 'ng' sound and a separate hard 'g' sound.
  • The 'G' Sound: Always a hard 'g' sound, as in giet (gate) or gud (good). It never makes a soft 'j' sound. For that, use 'j'.
  • The 'J' Sound: Always a soft 'j' sound, as in joj (judge) or jem (gem).
  • The Letter 'X': The letter 'x' is not used. Instead, we write the sounds we hear using ks or gz.
    Examples: ekspek (expect), egzakli (exactly).

Using 'Y' and 'W'

These letters have two important jobs.

  • As Vowel Separators: When two vowels that don't form a standard pair meet, use 'y' or 'w' to separate them so each keeps its own sound.
    sai-y-ans, uobi-y-a, fai-y-a, No-w-a, flo-w-az.
  • As Standard Consonants: Use them for their normal sounds at the beginning of words.
    nyam, kyan, yuuz, gwaan, wikid.

Note: You will not find 'y' at the end of a word to make an 'ee' sound. That sound always comes from the letter 'i' (e.g., foni, moni, dangki).
